mthfr is a recently recognized risk factor for numerous common diseases, such as hypertension, heart failure, vascular dementia, atherosclerosis, myocardial infarction, stroke, venous thrombosis, endometrial cancer, colorectal cancer, depression, bipolar disorder, and miscarriages. it is also associated with spina bifida, anencephaly, and homocystinuria. since the patient has two mutated alleles, he is at increased risk of developing these conditions. some of his symptoms may be related to early vascular dementia accelerated by his mthfr mutations, as there are some lesions on his mri that are consistent with this condition. he also seemed to have some mild cognitive slowing during the clinical encounter, which may be related to possible early vascular dementia from his mthfr. his cognitive symptoms may also be related to the mild tbi he sustained in his recent motor vehicle accident. symptoms such as headaches, dizziness, nausea, and comprehension difficulties are common in mild tbi patients, and the fact that some of his cognitive symptoms such as his word finding difficulties have resolved supports a diagnosis of mild tbi with post-concussive syndrome. another mri to determine whether the hyperintense lesions on his previous mri have resolved or developed into hemosiderin deposits may help distinguish mtbi from early vascular dementia in this case. if the lesions have resolved or developed into hemosiderin deposits, that would support mtbi with resolving brain contusion and if they remain, it would support vascular white matter changes that may represent early vascular dementia accelerated by his mthfr mutation. his new symptoms of eye muscle fatigability may also be a result of his mild tbi.  since fatigability with upward gaze is a common finding in early myasthenia gravis, an anti-acetylcholine receptor test may be warranted to rule out this disease.
