this case involves a 3 day old male who was born at 31 weeks and 4 days gestation, by spontaneous vaginal delivery.  the patient required positive pressure ventilation at birth and was immediately transferred to the nicu where he required intubation and mechanical ventilation.
this patient developed pulmonary interstitial emphysema as a result of being intubated and requiring positive pressure-assisted ventilation.  as a result of lung immaturity, there was a lack of surfactant.  the lack of surfactant caused the alveoli of the lungs to remain collapsed.  the granular pattern in the x-rays represents generalized alveolar collapse with various distended alveolar ducts and terminal bronchioles which are seen as air bronchograms.

as time went on, the terminal bronchioles and alveolar ducts became more over distended and were seen as small bubbles on the x-ray.  with simple rds, these bubbles disappear on expiration; however, in pulmonary interstitial emphysema the bubbles of air persist.  the emphysema develops as a consequence of the stiff, noncompliant lungs.

pulmonary interstitial emphysema occurred in this child after he was intubated.  in many rds patients, the assisted ventilation improves oxygenation; however, in some the elevated pressures result in air leakage from the terminal bronchioles and alveolar ducts into the interstitium and lymphatics.  this leakage of air results in linear (sometimes serpentine) and cystic bubbles extending to the periphery of the lung. 

complications from pulmonary interstitial emphysema include pneumomediastinum, pneumopericardium (may lead to cardiac tamponade), pneumothorax, and air embolism.  these complications can be fatal which illustrates the importance of identifying pulmonary interstitial emphysema early and the need to correct it as soon as possible.
