56-year-old female with past medical history of lbp, dm2, hld, and htn presents with right knee pain with vague descriptions of instability and “giving out” since striking her knee in an mva four months ago as an unrestrained backseat passenger.   she denies sensation of joint locking or catching.  ed evaluation the morning after the incident demonstrated normal findings on plain films of the knee and lumbar spine and the patient was discharged instructed to ice the sore areas.  one month ago, the patient had a three-day worsening cold painful right leg and right foot with decreased motion of the toes.  she reports seeking treatment at the ed.  she was started on heparin for thrombolysis and underwent catheterization and arteriography which demonstrated a patent aortoiliac system, bilateral slow runoff of the lower extremities, and occlusive thrombosis of the right popliteal, distal anterior tibial, posterior tibial and peroneal arteries.  she denies history of thrombosis, arrhythmia, coagulopathy, or bleeding diathesis.  current medications include acetaminophen and apixaban.  patient ceased tobacco and alcohol use 5-7 years ago.

the two most significant findings in this patient’s workup include: the osteochondral defect on the surface of the anterior lateral condyle with discontinuity in the femoral articular surface.  the other is right lower extremity ischemia, and associated arteriography indicating occlusive thrombosis.  

the various conditions that present with such a finding listed in the differential diagnosis are often referred to with the nonspecific term of osteochondral defect, which reflects the unclear etiology of more specific terminology.  from the information provided, we are sufficiently able to assess the underlying pathologic process and provide adequate treatment and referral for this patient.

the temporal factors in this case of sudden onset and persistence after trauma are more typical of a post-traumatic osteonecrotic process, such as secondary osteonecrosis, but the mri findings are more consistent with osteochondritis, which may more likely than not, based on location (see topic), not progress to end-phase osteonecrotic fragment.  there was no original subchondral bone fracture or disruption detected on two sets of plain film radiographs, further decreasing the likelihood of a pre-existing condition to the mva.  the pattern of mild subchondral bone and marrow signal with four months of symptoms is more consistent with osteochondritis dissecans.  in light of the sudden onset of symptoms after trauma, a single femoral condylar lesion, and absence of other predisposing factors (corticosteroids, alcohol, tobacco, sle, sickle cell, caisson disease, gaucher’s disease, thrombophilia, or hypofibrinolysis), spontaneous osteonecrosis is also less likely.  the location of the lesion is less typical for osteochondritis dissecans, but the lateral femoral condyle is the second-most common location.  

the contribution of the episode of acute limb ischemia with popliteal artery thrombosis could have decreased flow through the anterior tibial recurrent artery, but the abundant collateral circulation of the knee joint would likely maintain perfusion of the joint.  it is unlikely that this ischemic event produced the findings in this case, which corresponds more with the trauma to the knee.  it is possible that the patient had preexisting osteochondritis dissecans, which can be asymptomatic in earlier stages when the articular lesion is stable, but the patient’s age, location of the lesion, and timing of symptoms makes this less likely.

the mri finding in this case is consistent with a single stage iia (out of iv) osteochondral lesion, which portends a likely recovery.  if there is failure to improve, repeat imaging would likely be necessary to evaluate the osteochondritic lesion relative to  progression.  arthroscopy also becomes a consideration.
