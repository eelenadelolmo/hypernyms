pituitary macroadenomas are benign epithelial neoplasms composed of adenohypophysial cells and account for 10-15% of all intracranial tumors. tumors exceeding 10 mm are defined as macroadenomas, and those smaller than 10 mm are termed microadenomas. most pituitary adenomas are microadenomas.  morbidity  varies from incidentally discovered nonfunctioning tumors to disabling macroadenomas. morbidity results from mass effect, hormonal imbalance, and patient comorbidities. women of childbearing age are diagnosed more frequently with pituitary adenomas as compared to men, possibly due to presenting complaint of amenorrhea. incidence increases with age, peaking between the third and sixth decades. patients with pituitary macroadenomas may be asymptomatic or may present with complaints due to hormonal imbalance or mass effects, such as visual deficits, headache, elevated intracranial pressure, or intracranial hemorrhage
